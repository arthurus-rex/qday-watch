Understanding FIPS documents

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) is a US government organization that collaborates with industry and academia to standardize cryptographic algorithm implementations as Federal Information Processing Standards (FIPS). While these are intended for the US government and contractors, they are widely adopted in commerce and internationally. FIPS documents can cover many topics, but the ones linked below are specifications for PQC algorithms and are mostly pseudocode. Chapters 1-3 of these documents are background information and useful to all.

FIPS 203 - ML-KEM, the lattice-based key encapsulation algorithm intended to secure networking channels.

FIPS 204 - ML-DSA, the lattice-based digital signature algorithm to protect the integrity of the software supply chain through non-repudiation.

FIPS 205 - SLH-DSA, another digital signature algorithm based off stateless hashes. While it is standardized, it is not widely used and is not prioritized in burgeoning RFCs or in hardware security modules due to its size and computation complexity during operations. Because SLH-DSA is hash-based, its security guarantees are more grounded in long-term in-the-wild usage than lattice cryptography, and is intended as a fallback in case ML-DSA (and in the future, Falcon/FN-DSA) is found vulnerable. I do not actually consider FIPS 205 "required reading" for most, but provide it here for context and completeness.

Commercial national Security Algorithm Suite (CNSA) 2.0

U.S. National Security Systems (NSS) are required to implement a more strict subset of algorithms. These are defined by the U.S. National Security Agency (NSA) and, more or less, require the highest-strength cryptographic implementations possible. For example, CNSA 2.0 specifies that ML-KEM cannot be used as hybrid key encapsulation with classic cryptography, and must be used at Security Level 5 (ML-KEM-1024), equivalent to AES-256 in strength. This has bearing for non-NSS software, especially open source, because widely-adopted projects may encounter interoperability issues, but also must account for the computational complexity burden these security strengths create.

CNSA 2.0 (original) - Published September 2022, the original CNSA 2.0, but has since been updated.

CNSA 2.0 (updated) - Published December 2024, contains changes and addendums from the original document published two years prior. In true bureaucratic fashion, it is called "FAQ" instead of CNSA 2.1.
